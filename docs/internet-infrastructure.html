2. What the global Internet is

The public Internet is not a single network. It is a federation of autonomously operated networks (Autonomous Systems, ASes) that exchange traffic using BGP (Border Gateway Protocol), shared numbering (IPv4/IPv6), and common application conventions (e.g. DNS, TLS).

Layer Role
Submarine and terrestrial long-haul High-capacity links between continents and regions; often owned or long-term leased by carriers and large operators.
National and regional backbones Large ISPs and carriers aggregate enterprise and access traffic.
Internet Exchange Points (IXPs) Physical meet points where many networks interconnect, often via settlement-free or bilateral peering, reducing transit cost for mutual traffic.
Access and last mile Fiber, cable, wireless, mobile RAN, etc.
Overlay services DNS, CDNs, cloud, email—implemented on top of the layers above.

Operational takeaway. End-to-end reliability is usually a product of path diversity, contractual redundancy, mature operations (monitoring, change control, incident response), and understanding critical dependencies: routing, naming, certificates, time, and software supply chain.

3. IXPs and NAPs

NAP (Network Access Point) is largely a historical US term from the commercialisation of the Internet in the 1990s. The modern parallel is usually an IXP or a carrier-neutral meet-me facility where networks interconnect.

3.1 Economic models

  • Peering at an IXP. Two or more networks exchange traffic destined for each other’s customers (or downstreams), often without per-megabit settlement when volumes and policies align (“settlement-free peering”).
  • Transit. A customer pays an upstream for reachability to a large fraction of or the full Internet; smaller networks and many enterprises rely on transit for global reach.
  • Route servers. Many IXPs host route servers to simplify multilateral peering; each participant still applies its own import/export policy.

3.2 Physical setup and governance

  • IXPs typically live in carrier-neutral data centres with cross-connects, meet-me rooms, and a shared fabric (Ethernet VLANs or more advanced L2/L3 VPN designs, depending on the exchange).
  • Governance may be nonprofit (member association) or commercial; rules cover quotas, filtering expectations, abuse handling, and technical minimums.
  • Routing security on the public Internet is a community effort: IRR records, RPKI ROAs, prefix filters, and initiatives such as MANRS (Mutually Agreed Norms for Routing Security) describe best practices for operators—always implemented in compliance with local regulation and your peering agreements.

What IXPs do not remove. They lower cost and latency for eligible traffic; they do not eliminate dependence on transit for full global reach, on DNS and certificate ecosystems for many applications, or on legal and commercial relationships with facility owners and regulators in each jurisdiction.

4. Legal and compliance considerations

This section is most relevant when you actually operate network or facility capacity across borders. If you are reading Part I mainly to understand Part II, treat the table as why operators cannot treat the underlay as a law-free zone—not as a checklist you must execute yourself.

When expanding infrastructure across jurisdictions, explicitly plan for:

Area Typical obligations (illustrative, not legal advice)
Licensing and telecom rules Provision of network services, use of spectrum, or operation of certain equipment may require licences or registrations.
Data protection and retention Customer and traffic metadata may be subject to privacy law, retention limits, and lawful access regimes.
Sanctions and export controls Hardware, software, cryptography, and services may be restricted by origin, destination, or end-user category.
Lawful requests Subpoenas, court orders, and national security frameworks may compel disclosure or action; policies should be documented with qualified counsel.
Content and access policy Blocking, filtering, or carrying certain traffic may be mandated or prohibited depending on locale; align product design with counsel.

Technical resilience and legal compliance work together: jurisdictional diversity of entities and contracts can reduce correlated failure, but each entity must still operate lawfully in its own territory.

5. Building the operator underlay

Think in terms of dependency classes and lawful substitution: what can you diversify, own, or re-home if a provider is pressured or fails? This section is the underlay that Part II assumes is “good enough” for engineering decisions at the application layer.

5.1 Routing and connectivity

  • Obtain and properly document ASN and address space through RIR policy; publish consistent RPKI ROAs where deployed.
  • Use multiple independent transit providers and diverse physical paths (cable systems, countries) where contracts and regulation allow.
  • Consider IXP peering where it reduces cost and latency without creating a single irreplaceable chokepoint.
  • For multi-region services, consider anycast or geo-aware DNS, understanding that BGP remains a policy-driven system among willing peers.

5.2 Naming and trust anchors

  • Run authoritative DNS on infrastructure you control; use registrar and TLD strategies that match your legal and business continuity plans.
  • Public WebPKI depends on browser and CA policy; enterprise or customer-managed clients may use private PKI where appropriate and lawful. Design for transparency and key continuity with security review.

5.3 “Self-contained” software and operations

  • Define clear behaviour under partition or loss of global Internet (what degrades, what still works).
  • Provide multiple bootstrap endpoints and documented update channels; sign artifacts and verify integrity using processes that meet your threat model and legal obligations.
  • Minimise unnecessary third-party dependencies for the critical path of your protocol or API.

5.4 Non-terrestrial paths

LEO satellite services can add path diversity where terrestrial links are fragile. Constraints include capacity, latency, weather, terminal cost, export control, and commercial terms with operators who remain third parties subject to legal pressure.

6. Multi-region reference topology

A practical pattern for operator-scale services: at least two regions, each with multiple facilities and multiple transit relationships, IXP presence where it clearly helps cost and latency, a preferred private path between regions where affordable, and edge PoPs toward customers (anycast, DNS, or both). Observability should cover BGP visibility for your prefixes, RPKI/IRR consistency, and DNS health.

7. Building services assuming reliable connectivity

Working assumption. For the product and platform decisions in this section, treat last mile, IXPs, transit, and your own BGP/DNS setup as sufficiently reliable for the planning window: outages are short, bounded, and handled by your existing provider SLAs and redundancy—not chronic structural failure. You still design for region or zone loss (facility fire, misconfiguration, vendor bug), but you do not spend primary effort on “will packets reach the IXP?”

7.1 Capacity and ingress

  • Traffic and connection planning: model peak RPS, concurrent connections, upload/download asymmetry, and large-payload endpoints (e.g. proofs, snapshots). Size load balancers and WAFs accordingly; keep burst headroom.
  • Edge contracts: DDoS mitigation and CDN are usually commercial services—document runbooks for provider incidents and maintain a secondary vendor path where regulation and budget allow.
  • API design: prefer stateless request handling at the edge where possible; push session affinity only where the data model requires it.

7.2 Application tier

  • Horizontal scale: run application instances as cattle behind health-checked pools; scale on CPU, latency, and queue depth—not only on average load.
  • Async work: offload long tasks to queues and workers; define timeouts, idempotency keys, and dead-letter handling so partial failures do not corrupt ledger or billing state.
  • Configuration: centralise feature flags and config with audited change history; separate release of binaries from rollout of behaviour.

7.3 Data plane and consistency

  • Choose consistency per use case: strong consistency for financial or consensus-critical state; eventual models only where the product explicitly tolerates lag and reconciliation.
  • Replication: use multi-AZ (or equivalent) within a region for availability; add cross-region replication or backup for disaster recovery, with a documented RPO/RTO.
  • Backups and restores: test restores regularly; encrypt backups and restrict access consistent with privacy and lawful-access policies.

7.4 Security and identity

  • Authentication and authorisation: short-lived credentials, rotation, and least privilege for humans and service accounts; audit high-risk operations.
  • Secrets: use a managed secrets store; no long-lived secrets in images or config repos.
  • Supply chain: signed builds, dependency pinning, and vulnerability response SLAs aligned with your customer-facing SLOs.

7.5 Observability and SRE practice

  • Golden signals: latency, traffic, errors, saturation—per service and per dependency.
  • SLOs and error budgets: tie release velocity and change risk to measured reliability; page on user-impacting symptoms, not only infrastructure pings.
  • Incident management: clear severity levels, communications templates, and post-incident reviews with tracked action items.

7.6 Notes for blockchain-adjacent workloads

For systems like validator, relay, or API nodes that mirror chain state:

  • Separate consensus-critical paths from read-heavy API paths so API load cannot starve block propagation.
  • Document behaviour under partition: which APIs return errors, which degrade to cached read-only data, and how clients should retry.
  • Align peer discovery and bootstrap with the self-contained principles in §5.3 while staying within legal constraints on network use.

8. Honest limits

  • You cannot guarantee reachability to every customer if their ISP or their government blocks or degrades your service.
  • Public Internet routing is a consensus among willing participants; large adversaries can make peering or hosting uneconomic or legally impossible in specific places without attacking your power supply.
  • Payments, app stores, identity providers, and other adjacent industries form additional dependency chains outside pure “Internet plumbing.”
  • Part II’s assumption of reliable access is a design convenience; revisit Part I when entering new markets, during major provider changes, or when geopolitical risk materially shifts.
